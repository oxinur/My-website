Understanding the UK Employment Rights Bill 2024: What Employers Need to Know

The Employment Rights Bill 2024 represents the most significant overhaul of employment law in England and Wales since the Employment Rights Act 1996. For employers, the changes are far-reaching and demand immediate attention. Our employment law team at Kingsbridge Solicitors has prepared this overview to help businesses understand what is changing, when, and what steps they need to take now.

Perhaps the most widely discussed change is the proposed introduction of unfair dismissal rights from day one of employment. Currently, employees must complete a two-year qualifying period before they can bring an unfair dismissal claim. Under the Bill, this qualifying period will be abolished entirely. Employers will need to ensure robust performance management processes and clear probationary frameworks are in place to lawfully manage underperforming employees during their early tenure.

Flexible Working and Other Key Reforms

The Bill also makes significant changes to flexible working rights. Employers will face a higher burden when declining flexible working requests, and refusals will require demonstrable, documented business reasons. The time limit within which an employer must respond to a flexible working request will also be reduced from three months to two months.

Other notable reforms include: the strengthening of protections against dismissal connected to trade union activity; new rights for workers to receive a guaranteed hours contract after a defined qualifying period; enhanced protections for workers on zero-hours contracts; extended statutory sick pay from the first day of sickness; and new duties on employers to prevent sexual harassment in the workplace.

Employers should be undertaking an immediate audit of their employment contracts, handbooks, disciplinary procedures, and management training to ensure compliance with these forthcoming changes. The consequences of non-compliance — including Employment Tribunal claims and reputational damage — are significant.
