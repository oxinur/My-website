Why Provenance Matters: Our Journey to 100% British Sourcing

When I opened The Gilded Fork in 1986, I made a promise to myself and to my future guests: we would serve only food we could stand behind completely. That meant knowing exactly where every ingredient came from — who grew it, how it was raised, and why it mattered. Nearly four decades on, that promise has become the very foundation of everything we do at Marylebone's most enduring fine dining restaurant.

The word "provenance" gets thrown around a great deal in the restaurant world today. For many, it means little more than a decorative line on a menu card — "Cornish crab", "Cumbrian lamb" — without any genuine relationship with the producer behind the name. At The Gilded Fork, we do things differently. We visit our farmers. We walk the fields. We stand on the quayside at five in the morning and watch the boats come in. That relationship — that trust — is what makes our food taste the way it does.

Take our 28-day aged ribeye steak, which has appeared on our dinner menu in various forms since 1994. The beef comes from a single family farm in Herefordshire, the Whitfield Estate, where third-generation farmer James Whitfield rears a small herd of traditional Hereford cattle on permanent pasture. James and I have been working together for nearly twenty years. When he calls to say the cattle are ready, I know we're getting something exceptional. No menu card can replicate that story.

Our fish tells a similar tale. Every morning, weather permitting, our fishmonger Richard at Fowey receives a delivery from the day boats working out of Newlyn harbour in Cornwall. We specify exactly what we need — day-boat only, line-caught where possible, nothing that's been sitting in a hold for 48 hours. That insistence on freshness costs more, and it means some days the menu changes at short notice. Our guests understand, and they appreciate it. There's something thrillingly honest about a restaurant that tells you it's changed the fish course because the right fish didn't arrive that morning.
